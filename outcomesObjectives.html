Outcomes-based Planning Companion Guide

Introduction

Background

CMS-required outcomes are based on statutory or regulatory requirements and provide a baseline for what is required of a Medicaid Enterprise System (MES). These outcomes are outside of the Outcomes-based Planning (OBP) process. This document will focus on State-specific outcomes. State-specific outcomes are developed by states and should be measurable, achievable, and reflect the short-term goals of the MES project. Metrics provide measurable evidence that the outcomes are achieved on an ongoing basis. States are required to report on the system’s performance to CMS as a condition for receiving enhanced funding.

Purpose

Document what outcomes are, why you should use the OBP process to develop your state-specific outcomes, how outcomes connect to the MITA 4.0 State Self-Assessment (SSA), triggers and objectives for OBP, and the OBP process.

What is an Outcome?

Outcomes can take many forms and represent different things to different audiences. For OBP, an outcome is a well-defined, observable, and measurable change in the existing situation that focuses on the end impact of those affected. It expresses changes as benefits, by improving capabilities within an organization or for its stakeholders.

Characteristics of an ideal outcome: 1. Change – What are we changing? 2. Affected Stakeholders – Who and what will it impact? 3. Benefits – What are the benefits of the change? 4. Capability – What new or improved capabilities are required to make this change happen?

Example:
Improve provider satisfaction ratings by 15% within the next six months.

  • Change: Improve satisfaction with the Provider Enrollment approval timeline by providing additional interactive channels to resolve issues.
  • Affected Stakeholders: Providers, Provider Organization, State users, etc.
  • Benefits: Quicker onboarding of providers, provider retention, decreases state staff workload.
  • Capability: Enhancement to redefine the Provider Enrollment process such as incorporating real-time updates to the workflow and adding interactive channels to provide real-time issue resolution.

Purpose of the Outcomes-based Planning Process

  1. Consistent and repeatable process: Ensures outcomes are identified consistently across the organization.
  2. Sustainable: Can be applied regardless of the team or individuals involved.
  3. Transparency: Provides transparency into the outcome identification process.
  4. Traceability: Ensures outcomes are traceable to the original challenge or opportunity.
  5. Continuous Improvement: Ensures outcomes are regularly reviewed and refined.

Other benefits: - Efficient resource allocation - Improved accountability - Enhanced collaboration

How does the State Self-Assessment (SS-A) and OBP fit into the agency’s existing processes?

Using the Plan-DO-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle:

Plan OBP (Outcomes-based Planning) Define outcomes (State/CMS) and metrics; Identify the change, benefits, capabilities, and stakeholders
Do Planning Advance Planning Document (PAPD), APD, Planning & Execution PAPD/APD process for funding the project; Project / Program planning; Delivery (DDI/Enhancements)
Check SS-A, Operational Review Cycles, Certification Check and validate the outcomes using metrics
Call to Action (Trigger) Internal Triggers, External Triggers Capture gaps and prioritize (internal); Capture regulatory requirements and prioritize (external)

Objectives and Triggers

OBP is a strategic approach that places system improvements at the center of MES investments. It enables states to define the value their system investments are expected to deliver, track progress through measurable indicators, and demonstrate return on investment.

Key Objectives

1. Demonstrate the Value of System Investments

OBP articulates the value that system investments bring to state Medicaid program goals, improving operations, and delivering measurable benefits to enrollees.
Example:
A 30% reduction in provider enrollment processing time, enabling newly credentialed providers to begin serving patients more quickly.

2. Improve Program Performance Through Strategic Alignment

OBP aligns system investments with Medicaid program priorities and operational needs.
Example:
Achieving real-time eligibility verification in 90% of cases.

3. Strengthen Organizational Readiness and Capability

OBP ensures system improvements are accompanied by organizational conditions needed for success, such as change management and workforce development.
Example:
Decreasing average eligibility determination time by 25% within six months.

4. Create a Culture of Measurement and Accountability

OBP encourages a results-oriented management culture, where success is measured by tangible impact on Medicaid program performance and beneficiary outcomes.

5. Enable Consistency and Reusability Across State Projects

OBP supports a structured, repeatable approach to articulating goals and expected impact, promoting consistency across projects and enabling reusability.

Triggers for OBP

  1. Initiating a New System Modernization or Module Implementation
  2. Transitioning from Legacy Systems or Vendors
  3. MITA State Self-Assessment Results
  4. Modernization of Cross-Module Business Processes
  5. Shifts in Medicaid Policy or Strategic Direction
  6. Operational Reviews or Performance Audits
  7. Response to Ad Hoc Incidents and Corrective Actions

How Outcomes-based Planning integrates into MITA 4.0

MITA 4.0 incorporates TOGAF’s definition of a capability as the ability to accomplish a specific outcome or goal. OBP helps mature and refine capabilities over time. Capabilities are business-focused, emphasizing results and strategic objectives. By focusing on capabilities, SMAs can better align resources and processes with strategic goals, improving outcomes.

Outcomes-based Planning Process

  1. Identify the business challenge: Describe the “as-is” state.
  2. Identify goals: Main concern and blockers.
  3. Identify drivers and pain points: Tangible, real-world, observable.
  4. Summarize challenge and drivers: Business challenge statement.
  5. Develop desired outcomes: Changes/improvements/benefits for the “to-be” state.
  6. Identify audience and capability: Who benefits and what capability is needed.
  7. Summarize in Outcome Statement.
  8. Determine measurement: How to measure, monitor, and report progress.
  9. Define success: SMART objective.
  10. Map outcomes to MITA capability areas.
  11. Identify tasks and organize into projects.
  12. Track and assess progress.
  13. Routinely reassess and adapt.

Appendix B: Metric Guidance

Once a state establishes its outcome statement, it needs supporting metrics. Metric data is evidence of accomplishment. Well-designed metrics enhance transparency, accountability, and continuous improvement.

Considerations: - What quantifiable metrics demonstrate module/system meets outcome? - How do metrics assess program results? - What is the current baseline?

Examples: - Average time to screen a provider. - Number of ER/hospital readmissions (for preventive care services outcome). - Percent of patient engagement within 30 days after discharge.

Setting a timebound benchmark establishes a clear, measurable target for improvement.

Source: CMS MES Certification Repository: Writing a Good Outcomes Statement
